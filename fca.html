3.3 IXT -- The Ecosystem Unit

Core mechanic: 1 IXT = GBP 1 (fixed, always). IXT is earned by consumers through spending at participating venues. IXT can be spent at any participating venue. IXT is not tradeable on any secondary market or exchange. IXT cannot be purchased directly by consumers.
  • Earning: IXT is earned, not purchased. Consumers receive IXT as a percentage of spend at participating venues.
  • Spending: IXT can only be spent within the IX ecosystem (goods and services at participating venues). Venues set their own IXT acceptance rate (0-30%).
  • Cash withdrawal: Patron-tier members can convert IXT to GBP at an 8% fee, subject to a quarterly limit of GBP 2,000 and a minimum balance retention of 20 IXT. Explorer members cannot withdraw.
  • No secondary market: IXT is not listed on any exchange and cannot be transferred peer-to-peer (Phase 1).
  • Issuance control: IX maintains a minimum Ecosystem Fund cover ratio of 1.5x against all outstanding redeemable IXT. New issuance is paused if the ratio falls below 1.5x.
  • Burn mechanism: Transaction fees (50% burned), cash withdrawal fees (100% burned), early unlock penalties (100% burned), Business NFT exit fees (50% burned).

3.5 Business NFT (Fund Placement)

Each Z Partner contributes a percentage of its enterprise value (minimum 5%, no maximum) into the IX Growth Trust. This contributed portion is the "Business NFT". Key terms:

  • Contribution is a placement, not an equity transfer. At exit, the Business NFT returns to the venue at appreciated value.
  • IX manages 100% of the business operations regardless of contribution percentage.
  • IX takes 10% of the contributed Business NFT value (not 10% of the business), vesting over 24 months. E.g., venue contributes 15% of EV -- IX receives 1.5% effective stake.
  • Minimum placement term: 3 years. Target return escalates with tenure: Year 1 = 0%, Year 2-3 = 3%, Year 4-5 = 5%, Year 6-7+ = 10%. These are target returns based on expected fund performance, not guarantees. Returns are paid from fund distributions. If the fund underperforms, investors receive less.
  • Early exit fee: 5% in Year 1-2 of 3-year term only. No exit fee on 5-year and 7-year terms.
  • Consumer IXT earn funding: capped at 10% of total Business NFT pool, funded from venue revenue.

4. Specific Questions for Your Opinion

Q1 -- IXT CLASSIFICATION

Is IXT a regulated token, e-money, payment instrument, or unit of a collective investment scheme under FSMA 2000 and the Payment Services Regulations 2017? Key facts: (a) fixed at GBP 1, (b) earned through spend (not purchased), (c) spendable only within the IX ecosystem, (d) not tradeable on secondary markets, (e) not transferable peer-to-peer (Phase 1), BUT (f) Patron-tier members can convert IXT to GBP at an 8% fee (capped at GBP 2,000/quarter). Does the cash withdrawal feature bring IXT within the FCA's regulatory perimeter as e-money or a payment instrument?

Q2 -- COLLECTIVE INVESTMENT SCHEME

Does the IX Growth Trust (pooling Business NFT contributions from multiple venues, managed by IX, with IX taking a 10% stake and charging management fees plus carried interest up to 25%) constitute a Collective Investment Scheme under s.235 FSMA 2000? If so, which fund structure is appropriate: NURS, QIS, AIF? What authorisation does InitiativeX Ltd require to operate or manage this fund?

Q3 -- PARTICIPATION NOTES

Do the IX Participation Notes (fixed coupon 6-10%, asset-backed, 12-36 month term, GBP 5,000 minimum, with option to receive returns in IXT at 1:1 plus a 25% risk bonus) constitute specified investments under the RAO? Are they debentures, bonds, or alternative investment instruments requiring an FCA-authorised issuer or arranger? Does the IXT payout option (where the investor waives cash interest in exchange for IXT + bonus) change the classification?

Q4 -- PROPCO NOTES

Do the PropCo Notes (property-backed, first-rank mortgage, 8-12% fixed return, GBP 25,000 minimum, 24-60 month term) fall under the FCA's regulation of non-readily realisable securities or mini-bonds? What restrictions apply to marketing these to retail investors under COBS 4.7 and the FCA's 2020 ban on mass-marketing of speculative illiquid securities?

Q5 -- IXF UNITS

Do the IXF Units (fund equity, NAV-linked, 5-7 year lock-up, carried interest waterfall up to 25%) require authorisation under the AIFMD (as transposed into UK law) or as a UK authorised fund? Would InitiativeX Ltd need to be authorised as an AIFM (Alternative Investment Fund Manager) or appoint one?

Q6 -- DIRECT BUSINESS NFT CO-INVESTMENT

Does offering direct co-investment into a specific venue's Business NFT (Strategic tier: 2% co-invest, Principal tier: 5% co-invest + board observer, quarterly distributions) constitute arranging deals in investments (RAO Art.25) or managing investments (RAO Art.37)? Does limiting access to high-net-worth or sophisticated investors provide an exemption?

Q7 -- CONSUMER MEMBERSHIP AS GATEWAY TO INVESTMENT

Does requiring Patron status (GBP 30/month) as a prerequisite to access investment products create any regulatory issues? Could this be characterised as inducement or tying under the FCA's Consumer Duty (PS22/9)?

Q8 -- BUSINESS NFT TARGET RETURN ESCALATION

The Business NFT placement includes a time-based target return: Year 1 = 0%, Year 2-3 = 3%, Year 4-5 = 5%, Year 6-7+ = 10%. These are target returns based on expected fund performance, not guarantees. Returns are paid from fund distributions; if the fund underperforms, investors receive less. (a) Does the target return structure affect the regulatory classification of the Business NFT? (b) Is there any regulatory distinction between a "target return" and a "guaranteed return" that IX should be mindful of in its marketing and documentation? (c) What disclosures are required to ensure the target return is not construed as a guarantee?

Q9 -- FINANCIAL PROMOTIONS

Which elements of the IX ecosystem require FCA-approved financial promotions? Specifically: the Participation Notes, PropCo Notes, IXF Units, and Direct Business NFT co-investment. Can any be marketed under the exemptions in the Financial Promotions Order 2005 (high-net-worth individuals, sophisticated investors, associations of high-net-worth individuals)?

Q10 -- IXT CASH WITHDRAWAL AND E-MONEY

IXT cash withdrawal will not be available at launch. It will be enabled no earlier than 12 months after full platform launch, and only for Patron-tier members (8% fee, GBP 2,000 quarterly cap, 20 IXT minimum balance). When enabled, does this redemption pathway make IX an e-money issuer requiring FCA authorisation under the Electronic Money Regulations 2011? Can the fee structure, quarterly caps, tier restriction, and 12-month delay take IXT outside the e-money definition? What authorisation or registration must be in place before the feature goes live?

Q11 -- APPOINTED FUND MANAGER STRUCTURE

Q16 -- STAFF INVESTMENT IN EMPLOYER VENUES

IX intends to permit staff at IX-certified venues to invest in IX investment vehicles. Staff already receive IXT via the Employee Benefit Trust (EBT) as employer-provided compensation. The intention is to democratise investment access consistent with IX's stated ethos.

IX's proposed framework:

  2. Staff may invest in all other IX vehicles on the same terms as external retail investors: pooled Participation Notes (diversified across IX venues), IXF Units, PropCo Notes, IX Cashflow Notes (pooled, not venue-specific), and Direct Business NFTs of other IX venues (non-employer only).

  4. Employment documentation safeguards:
       (a) Separate, opt-in investor agreement distinct from the employment contract
       (b) 14-day cooling-off period during which any investment can be rescinded in full
       (c) No payroll deduction or salary-linked facility -- staff must fund investments from personal after-tax capital via the standard investor portal
       (d) Explicit non-coercion language on the offer document: "Your investment is entirely voluntary. Your employment, remuneration, promotion, and conditions of service are not affected by whether you invest, how much you invest, or the performance of any investment you hold."
       (e) On cessation of employment, staff investors retain their holdings on the same terms as external investors -- no forced sale, no forfeiture, no adjustment to lock-up or redemption rights.

Specific points for counsel to confirm, modify, or reject:

Q17 -- IX CASHFLOW NOTES (NEW VEHICLE E)

In addition to the four investment vehicles described at Section 3.8, IX proposes a fifth vehicle: IX Cashflow Notes. This is a short-duration working-capital loan instrument for IX-certified venues, modelled on merchant cash advance / business finance platforms such as YouLend, iwoca, and Funding Circle.

IX's proposed framework:

  1. Purpose: Working capital bridge for IX-certified venues. Use cases: seasonal stock-up, emergency equipment repair, supplier bridge finance, short-term staffing. Not long-term growth capital.
  2. Borrower: IX-certified venue OpCo (Ltd company). B2B lending only. Not available to sole traders or individual staff.
  3. Term: 3-12 months. Fixed at origination.
  4. Coupon to investor: 10-18% APR, graduated by IX Risk Rating (R1-R5 stage-based framework, see IX Risk Rating Framework v1.0). Maximum 18% to avoid classification as high-cost short-term credit.
  5. Underwriting: Based on 10-pillar certification score, historical venue revenue (from IX's POS integration layer), and certification tier. No external credit bureau check required for Layer 2 access; Layer 1 platform requirements apply if distributed via partner ECF.
  6. Repayment: Daily or weekly percentage of card takings (YouLend-style), or fixed monthly, at venue's election. Prepayment permitted without penalty.
  7. Structure: Debt securities issued by the appointed FCA-authorised fund manager. Investor buys the note (not a direct loan to the venue); IX services the loan. Pooled "IX Cashflow Fund" structure permits diversification across multiple venue loans.
  8. Minimum investment: GBP 100 via Layer 1 ECF partner; GBP 1,000 via Layer 2 direct (Class E); GBP 5,000 for direct venue-specific loan (Class C+).
  9. IXT-election: Subject to the Q15 framework -- up to 50% of interest in IXT for retail investors, up to 100% for Strategic / Principal tier.

Specific points for counsel to confirm, modify, or reject:

  • (a) Regulatory route. IX's preferred structure is to issue Cashflow Notes as debt securities under the appointed fund manager's permissions, rather than operating a peer-to-peer lending platform (which would require Article 36H permission and separate authorisation for IX). Please confirm whether this structure is viable, or whether the platform's active role in origination and servicing effectively makes IX a P2P operator regardless of legal form.
  • (b) Maximum APR. The 18% APR ceiling is intended to avoid high-cost short-term credit classification and to remain commercially viable for venues with 8-15% EBITDA margins. Is this ceiling correctly calibrated? Is there a Consumer Duty or TCF argument for a lower cap?
  • (c) Underwriting using proprietary data. IX (as platform operator) has real-time POS data from its integration layer. Using this data to underwrite the loan and price the risk gives IX an information advantage over external investors. Does this create any conflict-of-interest obligation, duty of disclosure to investors, or required firewall between IX's platform and fund-management functions?
  • (d) Repayment via card-takings percentage. The YouLend-style repayment mechanism (daily or weekly % of card receipts) is operationally attractive but may trigger FCA classification as a "business cash advance" with specific disclosure and fair-treatment obligations. Please advise on the regulatory characterisation.
  • (e) Layer 1 (ECF partner) compatibility. Not all ECF platforms list short-duration debt instruments. Which existing FCA-authorised platforms can accommodate a 3-12 month note with percentage-of-takings repayment?
  • (f) Retail suitability. Is a 3-12 month high-yield venue-linked note appropriate for retail investors under COBS 10A, or should it be restricted to Strategic / Principal tier? If restricted, what is the minimum tier threshold?
  • (g) Default and recovery. If a venue defaults on a Cashflow Note, what are IX's obligations to investors and to the venue? Can IX offset unpaid amounts against future fund distributions to the venue, or must recovery proceed through standard insolvency processes?

Q19 -- INTRODUCER / FINDER FEE STRUCTURE

IX proposes to compensate both itself and individual introducers (existing investors, Patrons, advisors, and professional intermediaries) for originating investment subscriptions into IX vehicles. The objective is a commercially attractive referral framework that is regulatorily defensible.

IX's proposed framework:

  1. Standard arrangement fee structure applied at point of origination, deducted from raise proceeds:
       - Participation Notes: 2% of raise (per existing Master v6.0 Section 9.A)
       - PropCo Notes: 2% of raise (proposed parity with Participation Notes)
       - Cashflow Notes: 1.5% of raise (per existing Financing Arrangement fee)
       - Development Convertible Notes: 3% of raise (new, reflecting higher structuring complexity and retail-facing risk controls)
       - Direct Business NFT: 1.5% of raise (per existing Enrollment Fee)
    All fees paid 50/50 cash/IXT per the Master v6.0 symmetric preferential-pricing rule.
  2. IX-sourced deals. Where IX originates the subscription directly (via its own platform, website, outreach, or existing relationships), 100% of the arrangement fee accrues to IX.
  3. Introducer-referred deals. Where an individual introducer (existing investor, Patron member, non-authorised finder) brings a verified new investor who subscribes, the arrangement fee is split:
       - Months 1-12 of the programme: 50/50 split (introducer / IX). Each side receives its share 50/50 cash/IXT.
       - From Month 13 onwards: 33/67 split (introducer / IX), reflecting standard UK finder economics once initial pipeline velocity is established.
    Introducer compensation is paid only on successful origination (funded subscription), not at offer or indication of interest. No retainer.
  4. Eligibility. Individual introducers must: (a) be an existing IX Patron member or approved professional intermediary; (b) register with IX as an introducer before making any referral; (c) not hold themselves out as giving investment advice; (d) not receive any inducement tied to specific product recommendations.
  5. Disclosure. Introducer compensation is disclosed to the investor at point of subscription in a standard-form statement: "An individual introducer is receiving a referral fee of X% in connection with your subscription. This fee is paid by IX and does not reduce your investment amount or return."
  6. Professional / authorised intermediaries. Authorised firms (IFAs, wealth managers, brokers) bringing investors are compensated under separate commercial agreements, not the individual-introducer structure. Standard IFA trail commissions and inducement rules apply.

Specific points for counsel to confirm, modify, or reject:

  • (a) FPO Article 25 exemption. Can an individual introducer (not FCA-authorised) legally receive a referral fee for arranging a subscription into a Development Convertible Note, Participation Note, or other IX investment vehicle without triggering the need for FCA authorisation to arrange deals in investments? Does the Article 25 "arrangement by non-authorised persons" exemption apply to introducer fees of this structure?
  • (b) COBS 2.3 and PS22/9 Consumer Duty. Does the proposed introducer compensation structure create a conflict of interest or inducement concern under COBS 2.3 or the Consumer Duty? In particular: (i) is the promotional 50/50 split (Months 1-12) defensible as a time-limited onboarding incentive, or does any preferential period create inducement risk; (ii) does the flat fee structure (paid on origination, not on performance) mitigate Consumer Duty "foreseeable harm" concerns.
  • (c) Disclosure requirements. Is the proposed point-of-subscription disclosure sufficient, or must it be: (i) pre-contractual and separately acknowledged; (ii) included in the Key Information Document (KID) if one is required; (iii) disclosed in the offer prospectus itself?
  • (d) Patron-as-introducer conflict. Patron members who are themselves IX investors may refer other Patron members. Does this create a market abuse or conflict concern if the referring Patron holds investments in the same vehicle? What cooling-off or disclosure requirements apply?
  • (e) Authorised intermediary route. For IFAs, wealth managers, and brokers bringing investors, is a separate contractual framework (not the individual-introducer structure) appropriate, and what form should it take (introducer agreement, distribution agreement, IAR arrangement)?
  • (f) Overall fee drag. When stacked with Layer 1 ECF platform fees (5-7% where applicable) and fund manager annual fees (1-2% p.a.), the total fee drag on Development Convertibles in Year 1 could reach 9-12%. Is this level of compound fee disclosure required under PRIIPs, MiFID II, or FCA CASS rules, and in what form?
  • (g) Recommended structural alternatives. If the proposed introducer-split structure creates regulatory risk, what alternative would counsel recommend to achieve the same commercial objective (financial incentive for existing investors and Patrons to bring new subscribers) -- for example, an IXT-only referral bonus paid from IX treasury, or a points-based programme?
Q20 -- PER-VENUE FOUNDING INVESTOR PREFERENTIAL RETURN

IX proposes that each venue-specific capital raise (Direct Business NFT, Development Convertible Notes for that venue, venue-specific Participation Notes) carry a ringfenced founding investor preferential return. This is a per-venue mechanic, not a network-wide preference class.

IX's proposed framework:

  1. Who qualifies. Investors subscribing to a venue-specific raise during that venue's founding round — defined as pre-opening and first 12 months of trading.
  2. Preferential rate. 6% p.a. cumulative, compounding.
  3. Duration. Until 100% of principal plus accrued preferential return is distributed. Not time-limited, but per-venue cashflow dependent.
  4. Source of payment. The venue's own cashflow only. Zero cross-subsidisation from other venues or pooled IX vehicles.
  5. After satisfaction. Founding Investors in that venue revert to pari passu distribution with all other investors in the same venue.
  6. Waterfall position. Sits as Stage 0 before Stage 1 (Return of Capital) in the five-stage carry waterfall set out in Section 3.10 above and in Master v6.1 Section 9.
  7. Pooled vehicles exempt. IXF Units and pooled Cashflow Notes do not carry venue-specific founding preferences — they are pari passu across the portfolio.
  8. IXT-election. Preferential distributions may be elected as 50/50 cash/IXT (retail, up to 50% IXT) or up to 100% IXT (Strategic/Principal tier), per the Q15 framework.

Specific points for counsel to confirm, modify, or reject:

  • (a) Venue-specific preferential class. Is a preferential return payable to one class of investor in one specific venue, ringfenced to that venue's cashflow, compatible with the appointed fund manager's AIFM permissions? Does this require each venue to be a separate SPV or can it be achieved through ringfenced cashflow accounting under a unified fund structure?
  • (b) Founding round definition. Is the "pre-opening + first 12 months" definition of founding round defensible under Consumer Duty (PS22/9)? Does this create an inducement risk against later investors in the same venue who missed the cut-off?
  • (c) Disclosure obligations. To what extent must non-founding investors in the same venue be disclosed the existence and terms of the preferential class before subscribing? Must this be in the offer prospectus, a separate risk factor, or both?
  • (d) Interaction with fund carry. The adopted waterfall places founding preferential return BEFORE all carry distributions (i.e. Stage 0 preceding Stages 1-4). Confirm this ordering is defensible under AIFM and LPA norms, or recommend a more standard ordering.
  • (e) Tax treatment. Is the 6% cumulative accrual (where not paid in the relevant period) treated as taxable in the year of accrual or the year of payment? Does this create a deemed distribution under CTA 2010 s.1000 or similar? What are the implications for investors on PIK-style accrued returns?
  • (f) Pooled vehicle disclosure. IXF Units investors have pari passu exposure to the fund's portfolio. Must they be disclosed the existence of preferential classes in adjacent venue-specific raises (that the fund may hold positions in)? What form of disclosure is required?
  • (g) Cross-venue investor tracking. A single investor may be a Founding Investor in multiple venues. Operationally, each preferential clock runs independently per venue. Confirm no regulatory issue with a single investor holding multiple preferential positions across different venues simultaneously.
Q21 -- MULTI-ENTITY VENUE STRUCTURE (BRAND HOLDCO + OPCO + PROPCO)

IX proposes a standard multi-entity corporate structure for every IX-certified venue, consistent with professional UK hospitality group practice (D&D London, JKS Restaurants, Soho House, Caprice Holdings all use variants of this structure). The full blueprint is set out in IX Venue Structural Blueprint v1.0.

IX's proposed framework:

  1. Brand Holdco. One entity per brand (e.g. "UKIYO Brand Holdco Ltd"). Holds all brand IP: trademarks, recipes, service standards, operating manual. Licenses brand to each OpCo via Brand Licence Agreement. Receives 2% royalty on gross revenue from each OpCo.
  2. Venue OpCo. One entity per venue (e.g. "Ukiyo Covent Garden Ltd", "Ukiyo Shoreditch Ltd"). Trading entity — employs staff, holds licences, books revenue, bears operational liability. Issuer of Business NFT. Payer of IX management fee. Assessed for 10-pillar certification.
  3. Venue PropCo (optional). Where required (property purchase via IX PropCo Notes, complex lease with significant premium, freehold ownership), a separate entity holds the property or the lease. Rents at arm's-length terms to the OpCo.
  4. Inter-entity agreements. Brand Licence Agreement (Brand Holdco → OpCo, 2% royalty); Inter-Company Property Lease (PropCo → OpCo, at open-market rent); Inter-Company Services Agreement (where central services are shared).
  5. IX engagement per entity. Business NFT issued at OpCo level; PropCo Notes (Vehicle D) issued at PropCo level; no direct IX investment at Brand Holdco level (royalty income accrues to operators / IX only at group-level R1 stage).
  6. Ringfencing. Each OpCo is a separate legal entity with its own balance sheet, P&L, and debt capacity. Failure of Venue 1 OpCo does not cascade to Venue 2 OpCo, to Brand Holdco, or to PropCo(s).

Specific points for counsel to confirm, modify, or reject:

  • (a) AIFM permissions coverage. Is the multi-entity structure (Brand Holdco + multiple OpCos + multiple PropCos) compatible with the appointed fund manager's AIFM permissions without requiring each OpCo or PropCo to be a separately authorised issuer of investment products?
  • (b) Single-issuer vs multi-issuer. Can the appointed fund manager issue investor-facing vehicles (Business NFT, Cashflow Notes, Development Convertible Notes, PropCo Notes) on behalf of each OpCo or PropCo without each individual entity obtaining its own FCA authorisation? Is the fund manager's permission sufficient as a single-issuer structure across the portfolio?
  • (c) Brand Holdco royalty. Is the 2% gross-revenue royalty from OpCo to Brand Holdco defensible as arm's-length under UK transfer pricing rules (TIOPA 2010)? What independent support is required (market comparables, valuation reports)?
  • (d) Brand Holdco VAT position. Does the royalty income trigger VAT at the Brand Holdco level? Is the OpCo entitled to recover VAT on the royalty as input tax? Any group VAT registration recommended or required?
  • (e) Inter-company lease arm's-length rent. Where a PropCo leases to an OpCo, at what frequency must rent be independently re-assessed to remain arm's-length? 5-year rent review standard or more frequent?
  • (f) PropCo Notes security. Where IX PropCo Notes (Vehicle D) finance property acquisition, what form of first-rank security should be registered against the property? Standard commercial mortgage charge or a tailored security structure?
  • (g) Change-of-control protections. What change-of-control protections should be built into: (i) the Brand Licence Agreement (to prevent OpCo sale without Brand Holdco consent); (ii) the IX Management Agreement (to prevent operator sale without IX consent); (iii) the Business NFT terms (to protect investors on a venue sale)?
  • (h) Insolvency waterfall clarity. In the event of Venue OpCo insolvency, confirm the order of claims: (1) secured creditors including bank lenders, (2) preferential creditors (HMRC, employees), (3) unsecured trade creditors, (4) IX management fees, (5) Brand Holdco royalties, (6) shareholder claims including Business NFT holders and Founding Investors. Any adjustment to this ordering?
  • (i) Tax efficiency. Is the proposed structure tax-efficient for (i) the operator, (ii) investors, (iii) IX Management Ltd? Any recommended modifications to improve post-tax returns without creating artificial or abusive arrangements?
